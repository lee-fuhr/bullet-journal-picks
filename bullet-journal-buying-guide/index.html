How to choose a bullet journal

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For a bullet journal in 2026, prioritize a notebook with dot-grid pages, bleed-resistant paper (at least 100 gsm), lay-flat binding, and an A5 size, it’s the sweet spot between portability and writing space. Budget-friendly options exist in softcover versions, while premium hardcovers offer durability and extras like numbered pages and multiple bookmarks. The best buy is whichever notebook you’ll actually use consistently, so match the features to your routine.

Is a dot grid really essential for bullet journaling?

Dot grid pages are the gold standard for bullet journaling because they give you a subtle guide for straight lines, boxes, and charts without overwhelming the page like a ruled or graph grid. That said, you can absolutely use a plain, ruled, or grid notebook, many experienced journalers do. But if you are new to the method, a dot grid will make layouts like habit trackers, calendars, and collections noticeably easier to keep neat. Some notebooks offer a dot grid with a faint margin line that doubles as a border, which many people find helpful.

What paper weight and quality should you look for?

The paper weight is the biggest factor in whether markers, fountain pens, or heavy ink will ghost or bleed through. A good rule of thumb is 100 gsm (grams per square meter) as a minimum; at that weight, most fine-tip pens and highlighters show only minimal ghosting. If you plan to use water-based brush pens or wet inks, look for 120 gsm or higher. Some premium notebooks go to 160 gsm, which nearly eliminates bleed-through but makes the journal thicker and heavier. Also consider the paper’s surface, a smooth, vellum finish works well for fine lines, while a slightly textured paper gives more grip for pencil and pastel media.

Which size should you choose: A5, B5, or pocket?

A5 (roughly 6 by 8 inches) is the most popular size for bullet journaling because it fits easily into most bags yet offers enough space for a full-page daily spread. B5 is about 7 by 10 inches, great for people who want more room per page or who journal with larger handwriting, but noticeably bulkier to carry. Pocket or A6 sizes are handy for on-the-go logging but limit you to very condensed layouts. Think about where you will use your journal most: at a desk, on a commute, or as a catch-all. Many people keep an A5 at home and a smaller companion for notes away from home.

Hardcover or softcover: which is better for daily use?

Hardcover notebooks provide a firm writing surface anywhere, better protect the pages in a bag, and often come with an elastic closure and two or three ribbon bookmarks. They tend to feel more substantial and last longer through daily wear, ideal if you carry your journal constantly or toss it into a backpack. Softcover notebooks are lighter, more flexible, and often less expensive. They can be easier to slip into a slim pocket or a flat bag pocket. However, softcovers may not survive a water bottle leak as well, and the covers can curl over time. If you plan to use your journal on the go, a hardcover with a sturdy binding is a safer long-term choice.

How many pages should your bullet journal have?

Most bullet journal notebooks run between 160 and 250 pages, which for most people lasts three to six months depending on how many spreads and collections they create. A thicker notebook (around 300 pages) can last longer but may not lie flat when you near the middle. Fewer than 150 pages means you will fill the journal quickly, which can be fine if you like fresh starts or seasonal resets. The key is to match page count to your journaling style: if you tend to set up many long-term trackers and monthly logs, go for 200 pages or more; if you prefer minimal layouts, a 160-page journal may hold you comfortably for several months.

What extra features are worth paying for?

Numbered pages and a built-in index are extremely helpful, the original bullet journal method relies on them for rapid logging and future planning. A two-ribbon bookmark lets you mark both your current daily log and your future log or monthly calendar. An elastic closure band keeps pages from getting dog-eared in transit. A back pocket is handy for storing loose reference cards or stickers. Many premium notebooks also include a pen holder loop, though those can be uncomfortable if you hold the journal in that hand. Avoid paying extra for purely decorative covers or gimmicky page designs; the utility features are what make a bullet journal truly functional.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use any notebook for bullet journaling?

Yes, the bullet journal system is a method, not a product. You can start with any blank or ruled notebook you already own. The only real requirement is that you enjoy writing in it. That said, dot grid and good-quality paper make the process smoother, so if you are buying new, those features are worth prioritizing.

What is the difference between a bullet journal and a regular journal?

A bullet journal is a structured system for rapid logging, task management, and habit tracking in any notebook, while a regular journal is typically free-form long-form writing. The bullet journal method uses short signifiers (symbols for tasks, events, and notes) and a modular layout that includes a future log, monthly log, and daily log. Many people combine both styles in the same notebook.

Do I need a specific pen to bullet journal?

Not at all. The best pen is one you already like writing with. However, if your notebook has thin paper (below 90 gsm), avoid heavy markers or gel pens that lay down a lot of ink, as they will bleed or ghost. For dot-grid layouts, a fine-tip pen (0.3 to 0.5 mm) keeps line work crisp and avoids smudging.

How much should I spend on a good bullet journal?

You can find perfectly good dot-grid notebooks for budget-friendly prices starting –15 from brands like Moleskine, Leuchtturm1917, and many Amazon Basics alternatives. Mid-range notebooks with thicker paper (120 gsm and above) typically cost –30, while premium options with special binding, 160 gsm paper, or design collaborations can run or more. The price does not guarantee quality for your specific needs, so focus on paper weight and features first.

Is it better to start with a simple notebook or a branded one?

Starting with a simple, inexpensive notebook is a smart choice if you are new to bullet journaling, you can learn the method and figure out what matters to you (like paper weight or page count) before investing in a more expensive brand. Once you know your preferences, a branded notebook with consistent paper and high-quality binding can be a rewarding upgrade.

What if I want to use watercolors or heavy wet media in my bullet journal?

For watercolor or wet-media techniques, you will need a notebook with paper weight of 200 gsm or higher, and even then, you should test the paper first. Some brands specialize in mixed-media notebooks with fine-art paper that can handle light washes without buckling. Be aware that these notebooks are thicker and heavier, and the pages may not be as smooth for fine-tip pens. Consider using a separate sketchbook for art and your bullet journal for planning.

In shortThe right bullet journal is the one that fits naturally into your daily life, whether that is a budget-friendly softcover with 160 pages or a premium hardcover with numbered sheets and 180 gsm paper. Start with a notebook that feels good to open, a pen you enjoy, and the basic rapid-logging method. You can always upgrade or simplify as you discover your own rhythm. The real value comes from showing up, not from the brand on the cover.
